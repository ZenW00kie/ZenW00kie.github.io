Summary

I am a PhD candidate in the Tufts Security & Privacy Lab (Teaspoon Lab) studying computer security, focusing on medical devices and Industrial Control Systems. My research looks at how tools and processes to ensure security for critical infrastructure, such as healthcare and the energy grid, are used and can be improved. During my PhD, I've worked closely with device maufacturers and network operators to understand their operating environments and ensure my research can directly impact their work.

NOTE: You may be wondering, "Ron, there's a lot of personal information here. Aren't you worried about this being used for social engineering purposes?". Sure, we need to be aware of and control the flow of information about ourselves online. My threat model accounts for this, and the benefits of sharing this information outweigh the cons. You can read about personal threat models and other interesting security things in my blog, Security4Humans.

Research Overview

My research focuses on the intersection of cybersecurity, critical infastructure, and human factors. In particular I am particularly interested in how to provide tools and processes to security personnel and ensure that security is a force multiplier to safety and reliability. While my career has been focused on providing data-driven recommendations to high-performance organizations, security has come up in one way or another. My initial foray into security professionally was when I was working with political campaigns, I was tasked with security operations as a sysadmin/the person with the most neckbeard. Later while on Wall St, I was investing in healthcare companies that underwent security incidents. As I observed this, the cogs in my head started to turn. Why is securing these types of systems difficult? What are the underpinning questions that impede secure-by-design systems?

A Ph.D. is about focusing on a very narrow topic area, and while some may think usable security for critical infrastructure is narrow, I focus further on two general security topics within this domain: threat modelling and vulnerability assessment. For reference, here is a inexhaustive list frameworks and standards I look at: NIST 800-53, NICE Framework, ISO 27000, ISO 10073, AAMI TIR75, and ISO 24392.

This type of research has me using similar techniques, such as survey design and statistical methods, for example ordinal regressions, that I used to examine how various groups make decisions (like what messages resonated with voters when I worked in politics). While my PhD gives me a knowledge of security, I would describe myself as a behavioural scientist or operations researcher looking at people and process challenges within cybersecurity rather than a cybersecurity researcher looking at creating tools and processes to solve challenges they have expertise and experience in. That said, I fully immerse myself in the problem space by interning with cybersecurity companies, participating in cybersecurity training, and engaging deeply with the security community. One of the most helpful learning experiences I had when working with political campaigns was going out and knocking on doors to see how my model predictions fared up against what voters actually care about.

Threat Modelling

Threat modelling is a way to abstract a security review to focus on the design of a system rather than the nitty-gritty details and also to think about threats and mitigations on a higher level. Some of the questions I think about are:

  • How is threat modelling done in practice?
  • What are ways to make recommendations to users about threats and mitigations to consider based on their experience and the context of the system/deployment?

My work on threat modelling is centred around how security professionals threat model, what are the barriers to adoption for threat modelling, and how we can formalize threat modelling.

Vulnerability Assessment

Vulnerability assessment consists of figuring out the risk posed by a threat or vulnerability in isolation and in the context of the deployment of the vulnerable software. The questions I am looking to answer include:

  • What are the various ways we can assess vulnerabilities? Are there specific attributes considered by medical device manufacturers or medical providers?
  • How can we improve vulnerability communication?
  • Can we "pre-triage" vulnerabilities using a threat model, i.e., can I rank vulnerabilities affecting the different aspects of my system based on the importance of those parts?

Purpose

When applying for academic jobs, you are required to provide statements on your research and teaching philosophies. Providing a general professional philosophy statement that drives my decision-making is essential. Maybe this is the self-centered Millennial in me that needs validation, or the influence of my mother who helps brands find their purpose.

Mission-Oriented

I've spent a fair amount of time working with the military and veterans in various organisations. Renny McPherson, a veteran and professional mentor, always talks to me about the importance of being driven by the mission. I've internalised this over the years and want to make sure that my work delivers on my personal mission to enable operators to focus on their mission by making security a force multiplier for safety and reliability.

Tikkun olam

In Judaism, we refer to a concept called "tikkun olam," which literally means repairing the world. Over time, this has grown, and for many now signifies a calling to a higher purpose and to do good in the world. My hope is that my work positively impacts society by minimizing security risks.

Do no harm

I've been on the front lines of figuring out how to leverage social media to influence voters when I was working in politics. The weaponization of social media, especially by foreign threat actors, has motivated me to spend time thinking about my role in this, and I strive to ensure that my work does not cause undue harm, or at the very least that I acknowledge the potential for harm.

Good design is integral

One of my most influential mentors, Scotty Riding, told me that good design begets adoption, and more importantly, lousy design will definitely kill it. He also introduced me to Ruby, whose design philosophy is to make programmers happy. While most of my software is written in Python, I consider this when designing APIs and User Interfaces.

My favourite colour is orange, and I chose Healthy Orange from Siemens Healthineers to emphasize important ideas, delineate sections, and site navigation. The colour echoes my work with medical device manufacturers, and is associated with promoting good health (security and otherwise).

Finally, my links are underlined in pink, used by Westminster School, the secondary school where I started rowing. The story behind the school using this colour is that Westminster and Eton, another prominent independent school in the UK, competed in a rowing race to wear red, representing the monarchy, in 1837. The Westminster eight won, but their uniforms had been dyed right before the race, and by the end, the dye had bled to pink. Because of the unexpected victory, the school decided pink must be lucky, and the colour was adopted. Since my time at Westminer, I've always thought of pink as a lucky colour.
